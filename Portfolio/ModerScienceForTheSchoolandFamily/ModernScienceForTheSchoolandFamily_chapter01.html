Chapter 1

MATTER

Section 1: Matter and Spirit - What's the Difference?

You've probably noticed that there's a big difference between physical stuff (like your phone, your desk, or even your own body) and the non-physical things (like your thoughts, feelings, and personality). We call physical stuff "matter" and the non-physical stuff "spirit."

This isn't just some random idea—it's something you experience every single day. You know instinctively that there's something inside you that's more than just flesh and bones. That something—your soul or spirit—is what makes decisions, has thoughts, and moves your body around. Your spirit is the real you, using your physical body as a tool.

Biblical Perspective

The Bible makes this distinction clear from the very beginning. In Genesis 2:7, we read that "the LORD God formed man of the dust of the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life; and man became a living soul." God created both the material (our bodies from dust) and the immaterial (the breath of life, or spirit). We're made in God's image, which means we have both a physical and spiritual nature.

Section 2: Alternative Philosophical Views About Matter

Throughout history, some philosophers have developed theories about matter and reality that differ significantly from the biblical view. Let's look at a couple of them so you can see why we believe these ideas are inconsistent with God's revelation.

Bishop Berkeley proposed a challenging idea that matter doesn't actually exist at all. He thought that what we think of as physical objects are really just impressions that God puts directly into our minds. So according to him, that phone in your hand isn't really there—it's just God making you think it's there.

There are several problems with this view from a biblical perspective. First, it contradicts common sense and everyday experience. Second, it contradicts Scripture, which clearly teaches that God created a real, physical universe (Genesis 1:1). Third, if Berkeley was right, why would Jesus need to take on a physical body (John 1:14)? Why would He perform physical miracles like turning water into wine or feeding 5,000 people with actual bread and fish?

Interestingly, Berkeley also believed that tar-water could cure all diseases. One writer joked that Berkeley "held two very odd opinions: that tar-water was everything, and that the whole material universe was nothing." This illustrates how departing from biblical truth can lead to conclusions that seem disconnected from observable reality.

Another philosopher, David Hume, went even further. He denied that the soul exists as an individual, responsible being. He claimed we're just "a bundle of perceptions" and that the "you" of today is no more the same person as yesterday's "you" than you are Julius Caesar or Cleopatra. Someone suggested this epitaph for his tomb: "Under this circular idea, vulgarly called tomb, impressions and ideas rest which constituted Hume."

This philosophy presents serious problems, both philosophically and practically. If Hume were right, nobody would be responsible for their actions because there'd be no continuous "person" to be held accountable. But the Bible teaches that each person is an eternal soul who will give an account to God (Hebrews 9:27). We are real, continuous individuals from conception until death and beyond.

The biblical view makes sense: God created a real, physical universe, and He created us with both physical bodies and immaterial spirits/souls. This isn't just ancient religion—it's the truth that best explains what we observe and experience.

Section 3: Why "Spirit" Is Called "Spirit"

Ever wonder where the word "spirit" comes from? It originally comes from words meaning "breath" or "air." Why? Because like the air we breathe, spirit is invisible to our eyes. You can't see the wind, but you can see what it does—it moves leaves, creates waves, and pushes clouds across the sky. Similarly, you can't see your spirit or someone else's, but you can definitely see the effects it has on the physical world.

When you move your arm, your spirit (acting through your brain and nerves) causes your muscles to contract. You see the result—your arm moving—but you don't see the spirit itself that made it happen. This is why Jesus compared the Holy Spirit to wind in John 3:8: "The wind blows where it wishes, and you hear the sound of it, but cannot tell where it comes from and where it goes. So is everyone who is born of the Spirit."

Section 4: How We Sense Matter

Matter affects our five senses in different ways. Some forms of matter can be detected by all five senses, while others can only be detected by one or two.

Think about water: you can see it (sight), feel it (touch), taste it, and hear it splashing (sound). Some materials have no smell, while others do.

Air is interesting—normally you can't see it, smell it, or taste it. But you can definitely feel it when the wind blows, and you can hear it moving. However, sometimes air does carry particles that we can smell. When you're near the ocean, you can smell the salt in the air. The salt particles are so tiny that no microscope can see them, but they're definitely there—they're matter floating in the air that stimulates the nerves in your nose when you breathe in.

The same thing happens when you smell a flower. Tiny particles of matter are released from the flower and float through the air. When these microscopic particles enter your nose and touch the smell-detecting nerves, your brain registers the scent. Even though you can't see these particles, they're real, physical matter.

Section 5: The Three Forms of Matter

Matter appears in three basic forms:

  1. Solid (like ice, rocks, or wood)
  2. Liquid (like water, oil, or juice)
  3. Gas/Vapor (like air, steam, or the smell from a flower)

Sometimes people group liquids and gases together as "fluids" and divide them into two types:

  • Elastic fluids (gases and vapors) - these bounce back when compressed
  • Non-elastic fluids (liquids) - these don't bounce back

Think about a soccer ball. When you kick it, it bounces because the air inside is an elastic fluid—it pushes back when compressed. If you somehow filled a soccer ball with water (a non-elastic fluid) instead of air, it wouldn't bounce—it would just go "splat."

Water can actually be both! When water becomes steam, it transforms from a non-elastic fluid (liquid) into an elastic fluid (gas/vapor). But for simplicity, we usually stick with the three-form division: solid, liquid, and gas.

Modern Understanding: Additional States of Matter

Scientists have since discovered additional states of matter beyond the classical three:

  • Plasma - ionized gas where electrons are stripped from atoms. This is actually the most common state in the universe! The sun and stars are made of plasma, and lightning creates plasma in the air.
  • Bose-Einstein Condensate - matter cooled to near absolute zero where atoms act as a single quantum entity
  • Other exotic states discovered in extreme conditions

However, the three classical states (solid, liquid, gas) remain foundational and are what we encounter in everyday life. The principles discussed in this book about these three states are still completely valid!

Section 6: Solids - Particles That Stick Together

In solid matter, the particles are locked in place relative to each other. Each particle stays in the same neighborhood—it doesn't wander around visiting other particles. This is what makes solids rigid and able to hold their shape.

This is more true for some solids than others. In super-hard solids like granite and diamond, the particles are absolutely locked in place and never shift position. But softer solids like gold or lead can be hammered, which changes the relative position of their particles. Rubber is a solid, but you can stretch it and bend it, changing how its particles relate to each other—though they still don't flow freely like a liquid.

Section 7: Liquids - Particles on the Move

The defining characteristic of a liquid is that its particles can easily move around and change their positions relative to each other. This is the complete opposite of solids.

When you move a solid object, like picking up a book, the whole thing moves together in the same direction. But liquids don't work that way. Pour water from a cup, and the particles immediately start moving in all different directions relative to each other. You can only move liquid as one unit if you confine it in something like a pipe or a syringe. The moment the liquid can escape, its particles start shifting positions.

Because wind and other forces are constantly acting on water, no water molecule stays in the same neighborhood for long. The Bible uses the phrase "unstable as water" (Genesis 49:4), and this is incredibly accurate scientifically! Water is never at rest. A single water molecule might be:

  • Floating on the ocean surface one day
  • Deep underwater the next week
  • Evaporating into the air and forming a cloud
  • Falling as rain
  • Running in a stream
  • Absorbed by a plant's roots
  • Released from a leaf
  • Sitting as a dewdrop in the morning
  • Flowing in your blood or in a tree's sap

Water molecules are always moving, always changing neighbors, always on a journey. This is one of the things that makes water so essential for life—its particles can move freely to transport nutrients and remove waste.

Section 8: Gases - Particles That Really Move

Gas particles move even more freely than liquid particles. Air is more unstable and restless than water. Even when the air seems perfectly still, the particles are constantly zipping around.

Here's an experiment you can try: Darken a room, but leave one window shade open just a crack so a beam of light comes in. Look at that beam of light and you'll see dust particles (called "motes") flying around in every direction. They wouldn't be doing that if the air were truly still—it's the moving air molecules constantly bumping into them that makes them dance around.

Air particles have an incredible range. The atmosphere surrounding Earth extends up about 50 miles! We don't know exactly how high water vapor can go, but it probably doesn't reach the very top of the atmosphere.

Section 9: How Liquids and Gases Fill Up Spaces

Because liquid and gas particles move so freely, they can squeeze into tiny spaces everywhere. They're always ready to seep into any substance that has pores or gaps big enough to let them in.

Soil isn't just dirt—it's a mixture of dirt particles, water, air, and various gases all mixed together. Living things, both plants and animals, also contain water, air, and gases throughout their structures.

Water makes up most of the sap in plants and the blood in animals. And air always goes along with water. When you breathe, part of the air you inhale actually enters your blood in your lungs and travels throughout your body. This oxygen is essential for your cells to produce energy.

Fish couldn't survive in water if there wasn't air mixed in with it. You can prove this with an experiment: Put a fish in a closed container of water. The fish will eventually die because it uses up all the oxygen dissolved in the water. In an open container, fresh oxygen constantly dissolves into the water from the air above, keeping the fish alive.

God's Design in Water

This is part of God's brilliant design—He created water with the property of dissolving gases like oxygen, which allows aquatic life to exist. Genesis 1:20-21 tells us God created water creatures on Day 5 of Creation Week, and He built into water the exact properties needed to support that life.

Section 10: Solutions - When Solids Dissolve in Liquids

When you dissolve sugar or salt in water, what's really happening? The freely-moving water molecules are able to surround and separate the tiny particles of the solid substance. The solid's particles get distributed throughout the water, mixing in with the water molecules.

Evaporation is actually similar to dissolving! When water evaporates into air, the water molecules spread out and mix with the air molecules, just like sugar molecules mix with water molecules when you make sugar water. So you could think of humidity (water vapor in air) as a "solution" of water in air.

Section 11: Heat and the Three Forms of Matter

Many substances can exist in all three forms—solid, liquid, and gas. What determines which form they're in? Heat.

Take water as an example:

  • Remove heat → Water becomes solid (ice)
  • Add heat → Ice becomes liquid (water)
  • Add even more heat → Water becomes gas (steam)

Different substances change form at different temperatures:

  • Alcohol has only been seen in two forms—liquid and gas. Nobody has ever frozen it solid.
  • Iron is normally solid, but in a foundry, intense heat melts it into liquid.
  • Mercury is liquid at normal temperatures but freezes solid in extremely cold Arctic winters. (This is why mercury thermometers don't work in super-cold climates—the mercury freezes! Alcohol thermometers work better because alcohol doesn't freeze at those temperatures.)

The difference between these substances is how much heat it takes to change their form. Mercury needs very little heat to stay liquid. Water needs more. Iron needs much more. But the principle is the same—heat determines the form of matter.

Perfect Design for Life

This is another aspect of God's design. He set the specific properties of each substance according to its purpose. Water freezes and boils at temperatures that are perfect for life on Earth. If water behaved like mercury or iron, life as we know it couldn't exist!

Section 12: What Is Matter, Really?

So what is matter actually made of? Can we say we truly know what it is?

We can observe what matter does and learn its properties, but even with the most advanced scientific analysis, we can't fully explain what matter fundamentally IS—any more than we can fully explain what spirit is.

Isaac Newton, one of the greatest scientists who ever lived, believed "that God in the beginning formed matter in solid, massy, hard, impenetrable particles." He thought this was true for liquids and gases as well as solids—in gases, these hard particles are just much farther apart than in solids.

This is probably true, but it still doesn't tell us what matter fundamentally is. It just describes how matter is organized.

Newton also believed that these tiny particles have remained unchanged since God created them. When something burns up, none of these particles are destroyed or altered—they just rearrange into new combinations. When wood burns, most of it seems to disappear, flying off as gas and smoke. But those same fundamental particles that were in the solid wood are now in the gas—they haven't been destroyed, just rearranged.

Conservation of Matter

This aligns with what we read in Scripture. God created matter "in the beginning" (Genesis 1:1), and the laws He established keep it functioning in predictable ways. The law of conservation of mass (that matter isn't created or destroyed in ordinary processes) reflects the fact that God created a stable universe that operates by consistent rules.

Changes in the forms of matter are happening constantly all around you. When you study chemistry, you'll learn a lot more about these changes and how atoms and molecules rearrange to form different substances.

Section 13: Atoms - The Building Blocks

These ultimate particles of matter are so incredibly tiny that no one has ever seen a single one, even with our most powerful microscopes. The smallest speck you can see with the best microscope is probably made up of millions or billions of these ultimate particles stuck together.

We call these ultimate particles atoms, and the theory about how they combine to make different substances is called the atomic theory.

Atoms of different substances aren't all alike—they differ in size and weight. Carbon atoms are different from oxygen atoms, which are different from iron atoms, and so on. We'll talk more about this when we get to chemistry.

Mind-Blowing Numbers

Scientists estimate that a single drop of water contains about 1.67 sextillion (1,670,000,000,000,000,000,000) water molecules, and each water molecule is made of three atoms (two hydrogen and one oxygen). That's about 5 sextillion atoms in a single drop! And each one has been perfectly designed and positioned by God to work together in precise ways.

Modern Understanding: Inside the Atom

We now know that atoms themselves are not indivisible (though the name "atom" means "uncuttable"). Atoms are made of even smaller particles:

  • Protons (positively charged) and neutrons (no charge) in the nucleus at the center
  • Electrons (negatively charged) orbiting around the nucleus
  • These are themselves made of even tinier particles called quarks and leptons

The original atomic theory was right that matter is made of tiny particles that combine in specific ways—scientists just discovered that those particles have their own internal structure! The conservation of matter still holds true for ordinary processes (like chemistry), even though we now understand the deeper structure. And this deeper complexity only makes God's design even more amazing—the precision at the subatomic level is breathtaking!

This atomic reality points to the wisdom and power of God as Creator. The complexity and order at the atomic level couldn't have happened by chance—it required an intelligent Designer. As Hebrews 11:3 says, "By faith we understand that the worlds were framed by the word of God, so that the things which are seen were not made of things which are visible." In other words, God created the visible world from invisible components (atoms and molecules)—exactly what science has discovered!

Section 14: Light, Heat, Electricity - Special Agents

There are certain mysterious things—light, heat, electricity, and magnetism—that some scientists think might be forms of matter, while others think they might be something else entirely (energy or force rather than matter).

If they are matter, they must be incredibly light and spread out, because countless experiments have proven that their presence never adds any weight to objects. That's why they've been called "imponderable agents" (imponderable means "unable to be weighed").

These agents are super important and incredibly active, causing constant changes throughout the universe. Two of them—heat and light—are obviously essential for life. Without light and heat from the sun, life on Earth couldn't exist. This is exactly what Genesis 1 teaches us: God created light on Day 1 (Genesis 1:3), before He even created the sun, moon, and stars on Day 4 (Genesis 1:14-19). God Himself was the original light source, and He created the sun to be Earth's ongoing light and heat source.

Modern Understanding: Energy, Not Matter

Scientists have solved much of the mystery! These "imponderable agents" are not forms of matter at all, but forms of energy:

  • Light is electromagnetic radiation—waves/particles of energy that travel at 186,000 miles per second
  • Heat is the kinetic energy of molecules—when molecules move faster, we experience that as heat
  • Electricity is the flow of electrons through conductors
  • Magnetism is a force created by moving electric charges

The original scientists were right that these don't add weight to objects—because they're energy, not matter! Einstein's famous equation E=mc² shows that energy and matter are related but distinct. The behaviors described in this book (how heat expands substances, how electricity flows, etc.) are all still correct—we've just learned the underlying mechanism of why they behave that way.

This discovery of energy as distinct from matter reveals even more of God's intricate design. He created not just matter, but also the fundamental forces and energy that govern how matter behaves.

We'll explore these fascinating topics in later chapters.

Biblical Worldview on Matter

From a young earth biblical worldview, we understand that God created all matter—solids, liquids, and gases—during Creation Week about 6,000 years ago. The matter we study today is the same matter God spoke into existence when He said, "Let there be..." (Genesis 1). The laws governing matter (like how heat changes its form) aren't arbitrary—they're reflections of God's consistent, faithful character. He designed matter to behave in predictable, orderly ways so that humans, made in His image, could study and understand His creation, and ultimately use it to serve Him and others.

Chapter 1 Vocabulary

  • Matter
    Physical substance that can be detected by the senses; anything that has mass and takes up space
  • Spirit
    The immaterial, non-physical aspect of existence; in humans, the soul or mind
  • Solid
    A form of matter with particles locked in fixed positions relative to each other; maintains its own shape
  • Liquid
    A form of matter with particles that move freely relative to each other; takes the shape of its container
  • Gas/Vapor
    A form of matter with particles that move very freely; expands to fill any container
  • Fluids
    Substances that flow; includes both liquids and gases
  • Atom
    The smallest unit of matter that retains the properties of an element; the building blocks of all matter
  • Atomic Theory
    The scientific theory that all matter is made of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms
  • Heat
    A form of energy that causes particles to move faster; determines the form of matter (solid, liquid, or gas)
  • Imponderable Agents
    Historical term for light, heat, electricity, and magnetism—substances or forces that cannot be weighed
  • Solution
    A mixture where one substance (solute) is dissolved in another (solvent), like salt in water
  • Evaporation
    The process of liquid changing to gas; water molecules spreading into air
