The Scientific Method
1 Ask a Question

Every scientific investigation begins with a question about something you observe in the world. The question should be specific and testable. For example: "Does the amount of sunlight affect how fast plants grow?" or "Which type of paper airplane flies the farthest?"

2 Do Background Research

Before conducting your experiment, learn what other scientists have already discovered about your topic. Read books, articles, and reliable websites. This research helps you design a better experiment and understand your results.

3 Form a Hypothesis

A hypothesis is an educated guess about what you think will happen in your experiment. It should be written as an "if-then" statement. Example: "If I give plants more sunlight, then they will grow faster." Your hypothesis must be testable!

4 Test with an Experiment

Design and conduct an experiment to test your hypothesis. Make sure to only change one variable at a time while keeping everything else the same. Record all your observations carefully and repeat the experiment multiple times to ensure your results are accurate.

5 Analyze Results

Look at the data you collected during your experiment. Create graphs, charts, or tables to help you see patterns. Calculate averages if needed. Does your data support your hypothesis or contradict it? Be honest about what your results show!

6 Draw Conclusions

Based on your analysis, decide whether your hypothesis was correct or incorrect. Explain what you learned from the experiment. Even if your hypothesis was wrong, you still discovered something valuable! Scientists often learn more from unexpected results. Share your findings with others and suggest new questions for future experiments.
