üëë Solomon Asks for Wisdom
After King David's death, his son Solomon became king over all Israel around 970 BC. YHVH appeared to Solomon in a dream at Gibeon and said, "Ask what you wish Me to give you" (1 Kings 3:5). Solomon could have requested anything‚Äîlong life, riches, victory over enemies, honor. But instead, Solomon humbly asked for "an understanding heart to judge Your people, to discern between good and evil" (1 Kings 3:9).
This request pleased YHVH greatly. Because Solomon asked for wisdom rather than selfish things, YHVH gave him not only unmatched wisdom but also riches and honor beyond any other king (1 Kings 3:12-13). Solomon's wisdom became legendary throughout the ancient world.
When two women came before him, both claiming to be the mother of the same baby, Solomon ordered the child to be cut in half. The true mother immediately cried out, "Give her the living child!" while the false mother said, "Let it be neither mine nor yours" (1 Kings 3:26-27). Through this test, Solomon's wisdom revealed the truth.
üìö Solomon's Writings and Wisdom
Solomon is credited with writing much of the book of Proverbs, the Song of Songs, and Ecclesiastes. Proverbs contains hundreds of wise sayings about life, relationships, work, speech, and godly living. Many proverbs contrast the path of wisdom with the path of folly: "The fear of YHVH is the beginning of knowledge, but fools despise wisdom and instruction" (Proverbs 1:7).
Solomon composed 1,005 songs and spoke 3,000 proverbs, and people came from all nations to hear his wisdom (1 Kings 4:32-34). News of his wisdom spread far and wide, and leaders from many nations came to seek his counsel, including the famous Queen of Sheba (1 Kings 10:1-13).
‚õ™ Building the Temple
But Solomon's greatest achievement was building the temple of YHVH in Jerusalem. David had desired to build it, but YHVH told him that his son would have that honor (2 Samuel 7:12-13). Solomon began construction in the fourth year of his reign, employing thousands of workers and using the finest materials‚Äîcedar from Lebanon, gold, bronze, and precious stones.
After seven years, the magnificent temple was completed. When the Ark of the Covenant was brought into the Holy of Holies, the glory of YHVH filled the temple so powerfully that the priests could not stand to minister (1 Kings 8:10-11). Solomon offered a beautiful dedicatory prayer, asking YHVH to hear the prayers of His people when they prayed toward this place (1 Kings 8:22-53).
üìñ Vocabulary
Wisdom (Old English wisdom)
The ability to make sound judgments based on knowledge, experience, and understanding; in biblical context, wisdom begins with the fear of YHVH and applying His truth to life.
Discernment
The ability to judge well; to perceive and distinguish between truth and falsehood, good and evil, wise and foolish choices.
Apostasy (Greek apostasia - stand away from)
The abandonment or renunciation of one's faith; turning away from YHVH and His covenant to follow other gods or teachings.
Consequence
The result or effect of an action or condition; outcomes that follow from choices, whether good or bad.
Idolatry
The worship of idols or false gods instead of YHVH; giving ultimate devotion to anything other than the one true God.

üèõÔ∏è Israel at Its Peak
Under Solomon's reign, Israel reached the height of its power, wealth, and international influence. The kingdom extended from the Euphrates River in the north to the border of Egypt in the south, encompassing territories conquered by David. Solomon established trade routes and formed alliances with neighboring nations, including treaties with Tyre (modern-day Lebanon) and Egypt.
He built a massive merchant fleet at Ezion-geber on the Red Sea, which brought gold, silver, ivory, and exotic goods from distant lands (1 Kings 9:26-28; 10:22). The Queen of Sheba traveled from southern Arabia to witness Solomon's wisdom and wealth, bringing lavish gifts of gold, spices, and precious stones.
üèóÔ∏è Building Projects and Their Cost
Solomon's building projects were extensive. Besides the temple, he built a royal palace that took thirteen years to complete, the House of the Forest of Lebanon (an armory), and fortified cities throughout the kingdom including Hazor, Megiddo, and Gezer. He also built storage cities for chariots and horses, establishing a powerful military force.
Archaeological excavations have confirmed Solomon's building activities, particularly the distinctive six-chambered gates found at Hazor, Megiddo, and Gezer. However, Solomon's wealth and building projects came at a cost to the people. He imposed heavy taxes and forced labor on the Israelites, which created resentment that would eventually contribute to the kingdom's division after his death.
He conscripted 30,000 men for work in Lebanon, and 150,000 workers for quarrying and transporting stones (1 Kings 5:13-16; 9:15-23). The people began to feel burdened by the demands of maintaining Solomon's luxurious court and ambitious projects.
‚úçÔ∏è Compound Sentences
Today we study compound sentences. A compound sentence contains two or more independent clauses (complete thoughts that could stand alone as sentences) joined together. The independent clauses are usually connected by a coordinating conjunction preceded by a comma.
There are seven coordinating conjunctions, easily remembered by the acronym FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So.
Examples from Solomon's life:
‚Ä¢ "Solomon asked for wisdom, and YHVH granted his request." (two independent clauses joined by "and")
‚Ä¢ "Solomon built the temple, but he also built many fortifications." (two independent clauses joined by "but")
‚Ä¢ "Solomon could have asked for riches, yet he chose wisdom instead." (two independent clauses joined by "yet")
When writing compound sentences, remember to place a comma before the coordinating conjunction: [Independent clause], [FANBOYS] [independent clause]. Each clause must have its own subject and verb and be able to stand alone as a complete sentence.

Today's Focus: We examine Solomon's tragic fall into idolatry and the lessons we must learn from his downfall. Despite receiving unmatched wisdom from YHVH, despite building the magnificent temple, despite knowing YHVH's Torah thoroughly, Solomon's heart was eventually led astray. How could this happen to the wisest man who ever lived?
‚ö†Ô∏è The Root of Solomon's Downfall
The root of Solomon's downfall was compromise and disobedience to YHVH's clear commands. YHVH had specifically commanded in Deuteronomy 17:16-17 that the future king of Israel must not multiply horses, must not multiply wives, and must not greatly accumulate silver and gold. But 1 Kings 10-11 records that Solomon violated all three prohibitions.
He accumulated 1,400 chariots and 12,000 horses, much of them from Egypt (1 Kings 10:26). He amassed enormous wealth‚Äî666 talents of gold came to him in one year alone (1 Kings 10:14). And most tragically, "he had 700 wives, princesses, and 300 concubines, and his wives turned away his heart" (1 Kings 11:3).
üíî His Wives Turned His Heart
Solomon's marriages were primarily political alliances with foreign nations, including Egypt, Moab, Ammon, Edom, Sidon, and the Hittites‚Äîall nations about which YHVH had said, "You shall not intermarry with them, for they will surely turn your heart away after their gods" (1 Kings 11:2). That is exactly what happened.
As Solomon grew old, his foreign wives turned his heart after other gods, and his heart was not wholly devoted to YHVH as David's heart had been (1 Kings 11:4). Solomon even built high places for Chemosh the detestable god of Moab, and for Molech the detestable god of the Ammonites, on the mountain east of Jerusalem (1 Kings 11:7). He did this for all his foreign wives who burned incense and sacrificed to their gods.
‚öñÔ∏è YHVH's Judgment
YHVH's anger burned against Solomon because his heart had turned away despite YHVH appearing to him twice and specifically commanding him not to go after other gods (1 Kings 11:9-10). As judgment, YHVH declared that He would tear the kingdom away from Solomon's son, though for David's sake He would leave one tribe (1 Kings 11:11-13).
YHVH also raised up adversaries against Solomon during his lifetime, including Hadad the Edomite and Jeroboam the son of Nebat, signaling the coming division (1 Kings 11:14, 26). The book of Ecclesiastes is traditionally understood as Solomon's reflections in his old age, after experiencing the emptiness of pursuing everything the world offers‚Äîwisdom, pleasure, wealth, accomplishments‚Äîwhile forsaking wholehearted devotion to YHVH.
The repeated refrain "vanity of vanities, all is vanity" (Ecclesiastes 1:2) expresses the futility of life lived apart from YHVH's purposes. The conclusion of the book is sobering: "Fear God and keep His commandments, for this is the whole duty of man. For God will bring every deed into judgment, including every hidden thing, whether it is good or evil" (Ecclesiastes 12:13-14).

Final Review: As we conclude our study of Solomon, we must soberly reflect on the tragedy of his life. Solomon started with such promise‚Äîwisdom from YHVH, international fame, the honor of building the temple, wealth and honor beyond measure. Yet he ended his life as a builder of idolatrous high places, with a heart turned away from YHVH. How does someone with so much wisdom make such foolish choices?
üí° Lessons from Solomon's Life
The answer lies in the difference between knowledge and obedience. Solomon had knowledge‚Äîunmatched wisdom and understanding. But wisdom is worthless if it is not applied. James 1:22 says, "Prove yourselves doers of the word, and not merely hearers who delude themselves." Solomon knew what YHVH commanded, but he chose his own path.
He rationalized his disobedience‚Äîperhaps he told himself that the political marriages were necessary for peace, that the wealth was for YHVH's glory, that he was wise enough to avoid being influenced by pagan wives. But YHVH's Word is not a suggestion to be weighed against our circumstances; it is an absolute standard to be obeyed.
Solomon's fall also demonstrates that no one is immune to temptation, pride, and compromise. If the wisest man who ever lived could fall into idolatry, how much more must we guard our hearts! Proverbs 4:23 says, "Watch over your heart with all diligence, for from it flow the springs of life." Solomon wrote those words, but he failed to heed his own wisdom in his later years.
‚úùÔ∏è One Greater Than Solomon
Finally, Solomon's life points us to one greater than Solomon‚ÄîY'eshua the Messiah. In Matthew 12:42, Y'eshua said, "The Queen of the South will rise up with this generation at the judgment and will condemn it, because she came from the ends of the earth to hear the wisdom of Solomon; and behold, something greater than Solomon is here."
Y'eshua is the wisdom of YHVH personified (1 Corinthians 1:24, 30). He never compromised, never fell into sin, and perfectly obeyed the Father in all things. Where Solomon failed, Y'eshua succeeded. He is the true King who reigns forever in righteousness and truth.
